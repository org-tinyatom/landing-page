# TinyAtom Marketing Channels

**Purpose:** Where and how to reach TinyAtom's beachhead audience — startup teams — and the positioning to use in every channel. This reflects the July 2026 repositioning of the landing page (see the homepage and `llms.txt`).

**Companion docs:** [positioning-businesses-vs-individuals](../marketing/positioning-businesses-vs-individuals.md) · [distribution-channels](../marketing/distribution-channels.md) · [monetization-model](../marketing/monetization-model.md)

---

## 1. The audience

Tech startups are a better initial audience than traditional small businesses, especially while the product is a macOS beta. But "tech startups" is still too broad. The beachhead:

> **Technical founders and engineering leaders at 5–30 person startups who need small internal tools but do not want to maintain another production app.**

Narrowed further, the first push targets:

> Technical founders of **bootstrapped and seed-stage B2B SaaS startups with 3–20 employees**, using Macs, who are already building small internal scripts and tools with AI.

Why this audience works:

- TinyAtom's promise — build local internal tools without servers, deployments, or update infrastructure — fits teams that understand the cost of creating "just one small internal dashboard."
- The product currently requires Apple-silicon Macs, which are common in early-stage technical teams.
- The audience is narrow enough to reach, understands the product immediately, and can tolerate an early beta.

## 2. Best startup use cases

Do **not** compete with Retool or large cloud-based admin platforms yet. Focus on tools that are:

- Used by one person or a small team
- Too specific for existing SaaS
- Not important enough to enter the engineering roadmap
- Better kept locally
- Built from CSVs, files, forms, or API data

Examples:

| Tool | Job it replaces |
| --- | --- |
| Customer-feedback organizer | Feedback scattered across spreadsheets and inboxes |
| User-interview repository | Interview notes lost in shared docs |
| QA testing checklist | Ad-hoc pre-release checks |
| Incident-review organizer | Postmortems in loose docs |
| Release-readiness dashboard | "Are we ready to ship?" threads |
| App-store review analyzer | Manually skimming reviews |
| Investor-update generator | Rebuilding the same update every month |
| Candidate comparison tool | Hiring notes with no consistent rubric |
| Local support-ticket analyzer | Exported CSVs nobody looks at |
| Sales-call / customer-request tracker | Founder-led sales in a spreadsheet |

The strongest trigger:

> **"We need this internally, but it is not worth assigning an engineer to it."**

## 3. Positioning

**Avoid:**

> Build internal tools without servers.

**Use:**

> Build the internal tools your startup needs without putting them on the engineering roadmap.

Or:

> Turn your startup's spreadsheets, scripts, and manual workflows into local desktop tools.

A generic "internal tool builder" is harder to understand than:

> Build the tiny tools your startup needs, without adding them to the engineering backlog.

## 4. Channels, ranked

### 4.1 X — the best daily channel

Existing posting about AI coding and software architecture gives a natural audience fit.

**Search and interact around phrases such as:**

- "internal tool"
- "built a quick script"
- "engineering backlog"
- "manual workflow"
- "using a spreadsheet for"
- "founder-led sales"
- "customer feedback"
- "vibe coded"
- "weekend project"

**Post specific builds, not generic TinyAtom promotion:**

> Our startup needed a simple place to organize customer requests. It wasn't worth adding to the engineering roadmap, so I built a local desktop tool for it with TinyAtom.

Then show a 20–40 second demo.

### 4.2 Founder and builder communities

Indie Hackers is explicitly built around developers and founders sharing their products, strategies, and progress. Prioritize:

- Indie Hackers
- Hacker News
- r/SaaS
- r/startups
- r/indiehackers
- Founder Discord and Slack communities
- Local startup meetups
- Accelerator alumni groups already accessible

Do **not** enter with "Here is my product." Ask:

> What internal tool does your startup need, but keeps postponing because customer-facing work always comes first?

That question yields both leads and template ideas.

### 4.3 Direct outreach to recently funded startups

Probably the best **controlled** acquisition channel. Look for startups that:

- Have 5–30 employees
- Raised pre-seed or seed funding
- Are hiring operations, support, sales, or customer-success staff
- Have fewer than ~10 engineers
- Use Macs
- Recently launched their product

Contact the founder, CTO, head of operations, or first customer-success hire. The message should focus on a **workflow, not the platform**:

> I'm building TinyAtom for startup teams that need small internal tools but don't want another app in their engineering backlog. What workflow is your team still handling through spreadsheets, scripts, or shared docs?

Offer to build their first tool with them.

### 4.4 Product Hunt — a launch event, not an ongoing channel

Product Hunt can give TinyAtom awareness, but competition for attention among startup tools is substantial. Launch when someone can, without assistance:

1. Download TinyAtom.
2. Choose a recognizable startup template.
3. Customize it quickly.
4. See value on their own.

### 4.5 Show HN — technical credibility

TinyAtom has characteristics Hacker News readers appreciate:

- Local-first
- No server required
- Developer-adjacent
- Desktop software
- A clear technical philosophy

A good title:

> Show HN: TinyAtom – Build small internal tools that run locally, without servers

Lead with technical substance: architecture, security model, sharing model, limitations, and why it was built. HN exposure can significantly increase attention for technical projects, but results vary heavily with launch quality and timing.

## 5. The first distribution loop

> Build one useful startup Atom → share the demo on X → offer the template → interview installers → improve it → build the next adjacent Atom.

Repeat until a template reliably converts strangers into installers, then scale that template's channel.
